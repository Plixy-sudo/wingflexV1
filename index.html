Aerodynamics

What is Wingflex?

Wing flexibility is not a flaw; it is a feature. Modern aircraft wings are designed to bend significantly during flight. This flexibility acts like a giant shock absorber, smoothing out turbulence and reducing structural fatigue on the fuselage.

Understanding G-Forces

When an airplane turns or pulls up, the wings must support more than just the aircraft's weight. This multiplier is called the "Load Factor" or G-Force. A 60-degree bank angle results in 2Gs, meaning the wings are supporting twice the plane's weight.

Engineering

The Dreamliner Effect

The Boeing 787 Dreamliner is famous for its composite wings. Carbon fiber allows for a much higher aspect ratio and extreme flexibility. In static tests, the wing tips were bent 25 feet upward before failure, proving immense structural integrity.

Why Wings Don't Snap

It is a common fear among nervous flyers: seeing the wing bounce in turbulence. However, aeronautical engineers design wings with a "Limit Load" and an "Ultimate Load".


Commercial aircraft are tested to withstand 150% of the highest load ever expected in service. The wing spars, ribs, and skin work together to distribute stress evenly, allowing the wing to bend rather than break.
